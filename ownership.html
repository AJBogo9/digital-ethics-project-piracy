Digital Ownership

Author

Oskari Hiedanpää

The ethical question

When you pay full price to buy something digital, can you truly own it or are you just renting access until the seller decides otherwise? What you “buy” is usually a licence to access the content, not the content itself (Electronic Frontier Foundation, n.d.; Hebert, 2024). Most content nowadays is only available digitally, or is much easier to get digitally, so you effectively have to buy the digital versions. In this section we examine if the methods companies use in selling their digital products to consumers are really ethical.

Rent vs. own

Subscription Digital “purchase” Physical copy
Pay once? No Yes Yes
Revocable? Yes Yes No
Survives platform shutdown? No No Yes
Resell/lend? No No Yes
Updatable? Yes Yes No

(Electronic Frontier Foundation, n.d.; Hebert, 2024)

Platform dependency

  • Your library is tied to one platform’s app and account
  • You cannot move it to a competitor or export it
  • You cannot resell or lend individual items (Electronic Frontier Foundation, n.d.)
  • If you leave the platform, your library leaves you
  • If the platform gets shut down, your library gets shut down (European Citizens’ Initiative, 2024)

None of these issues exist with physical media

Arguments for

The terms are disclosed and buyers agree to them

  • The user has to accept the EULA, which means they know what they sign up for (Electronic Frontier Foundation, n.d.)

Digital Rights Management (DRM) technologies protect creators from piracy

  • Prevent you from sharing copies of your downloads
  • Check the validity of game files by connecting to online servers (King, 2026)

Digital can be cheaper and is more convenient.

  • If companies don’t have to pay for manufacturing and distribution, the price can be deducted from the final product
  • Getting access to games and movies straight from home is a modern luxury

Being online enables updates

  • Developers can patch bugs and give more content through the internet

Arguments against

The terms are huge papers that no one has the time to read

  • Studies confirm that they are accepted in seconds without further reading (Obar & Oeldorf-Hirsch, 2020)

DRMs sometimes have incompatibility issues

  • If you want to play a game with bad internet, you probably can’t (King, 2026)
  • Some Anti-Cheats don’t work on all platforms, for example Windows vs Linux
  • DRMs are usually cracked on the first day anyway, so adding them just hurts the lawful consumers (Electronic Frontier Foundation, n.d.) (see CD Projekt Red’s response to this in The Creator’s Perspective)

Digital is easier to take away than physical

  • You can never be sure when a platform shuts down, or the developer just decides to not support a game anymore (European Citizens’ Initiative, 2024)
  • Physical can only be stolen physically, which is much less common

Updating shouldn’t require you to be online 24/7

  • Allow updating when you feel like it and don’t force you to always be online if there just happens to be a new update just now (King, 2026)

Ethical questions

  • If a seller can revoke what you bought, is calling it a purchase honest, and does that make piracy self-defense, or just a convenient excuse?
  • If the terms are unfair, is the ethical response to refuse to buy, or to take the work anyway?
  • Is consent that nobody reads still real consent?
  • The intrusive DRM comes from the platform, but piracy hits the creator. Is it fair to punish the developer for the store’s choices?
  • Is there a happy medium where all sides are satisfied?
