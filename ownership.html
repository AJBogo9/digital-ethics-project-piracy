The ethical question

When you pay full price to buy something digital, can you truly own it — or are you just renting access until the seller decides otherwise? This section examines the gap between what “purchasing” digital media promises and what it actually delivers, and why that gap matters for the ethics of piracy.

Core thesis

Clicking “Buy” on a digital storefront hands you a revocable licence, not a property right. The Federal Trade Commission warned in 2024 that the word buy can be actively misleading when the seller retains the right to take access away (FTC, 2024). Most consumers don’t realise the swap from ownership to licencing has happened.

Subscriptions are at least honest — you pay to keep access. One-time digital purchases borrow ownership’s vocabulary and price point while delivering something much closer to a rental.

Platform dependency & lock-in

  • Your library is tied to one platform’s app and account
  • You cannot move it to a competitor or export it
  • You cannot resell or lend individual items
  • If you leave the platform (or the platform leaves you), the library goes with it

All of these were taken for granted with physical media.

When platforms die: The Crew

Ubisoft’s racing game The Crew was sold as a purchase to millions of players. Because it required a persistent server connection even for solo play, when Ubisoft shut those servers off on 31 March 2024, the game became completely unplayable — not degraded, not offline-only, simply gone.

French consumer group UFC-Que Choisir and a California class action both sued Ubisoft, alleging buyers had been misled into thinking they owned the game (Game Developer, 2024). The case attracted backing from the Stop Killing Games initiative, which had gathered over 1.3 million signatures arguing that publishers should not be allowed to permanently disable purchased software (Wikipedia, 2024).

The legal mechanism: first-sale doctrine

Copyright law was designed around physical copies and included a consumer-protective balance: the first-sale doctrine, which lets you resell, lend, or give away a copy you lawfully bought. That is what makes second-hand bookshops and libraries legal.

Digital distribution exploits the gap. In Capitol Records v. ReDigi (2018), courts held that first-sale doctrine does not extend to digital files — transferring a file necessarily creates a new copy, which is the copyright holder’s exclusive right. Sellers then layer licence agreements on top of that gap, stripping consumers of resale and transfer rights without ever announcing the loss.

Counter-arguments

“The terms are disclosed — buyers agreed.” EULA terms are buried, unreadable, and non-negotiable. Nominal consent to a 50-page agreement written by the seller’s lawyers is not meaningful informed consent.

“DRM and always-online protect creators from piracy.” That may justify technical measures — it does not justify labelling a revocable licence as a purchase.

“Digital is cheaper and more convenient.” Convenience is a genuine benefit. Mislabelling the product is a separate issue.

“Always-online enables updates and anti-cheat.” Those are design choices with trade-offs. The harm is not the design choice itself but selling the result as permanent ownership.

Why it matters ethically

  • Honesty: consumers are charged ownership prices and given ownership language, but receive a revocable licence
  • Informed consent: the difference between what is sold and what is delivered is not communicated clearly
  • Preservation: when a paid-for library can be erased by a corporate decision, personal archiving starts to look less like theft and more like self-defence

This doesn’t make piracy morally right. But it does mean the moral weight that rights-holders invoke against piracy — the appeal to a fair exchange honestly entered into — is partly undermined by their own side of the bargain.
