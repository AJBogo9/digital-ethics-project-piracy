The Creator’s Perspective

Author

Andreas

Introduction

  • Piracy is often framed as a consumer issue, but creators sit at the ethical core of the debate
  • Creators invest time, money, and creative labor into their work – piracy directly affects the return on that investment
  • The ethical question: does the public’s interest in access outweigh the creator’s right to control and profit from their work?
  • Two distinct categories of rights apply: economic rights (the right to profit from your work) and moral rights (the right to control how your work is used and attributed), both recognised under the Berne Convention (World Intellectual Property Organization 1886)

Economic Rights vs. Moral Rights

  • Copyright grants creators two types of rights under the Berne Convention (Article 6bis) (World Intellectual Property Organization 1886):
    • Economic rights: the right to be compensated for use of one’s work; these can be transferred or licensed
    • Moral rights: the right to claim authorship and object to modifications that harm reputation; these cannot be transferred or waived
  • Piracy primarily undermines economic rights, but can also affect moral rights (e.g., modified or cracked versions of a work distributed without the creator’s consent) (Wikipedia contributors 2024b)
  • The US copyright tradition is rooted almost entirely in economic rights, while the European/French tradition (“droits d’auteur”) places greater weight on moral rights – this shapes how different legal systems treat piracy (Wikipedia contributors 2024b)

Ethical Frameworks

Three established philosophical frameworks illuminate why this debate is genuinely contested rather than easily resolved:

  • Locke’s labour theory of property provides the philosophical foundation of copyright itself: when a person mixes their labour with raw material, the result becomes their property (Locke 1689). A game developer who spends years writing code and designing systems has mixed their labour with ideas; the resulting work is rightfully theirs. Piracy takes that product without consent – a violation of a natural property right, not merely a legal one.
  • Kantian deontology offers two independent reasons why piracy is ethically problematic regardless of consequences (UBC Allard School of Law – Social Media Law 2024):
    • Universalisability: if everyone pirated, creators would lose the income to keep creating – the very content being pirated would cease to exist. Piracy cannot be universalised without undermining itself.
    • Treating persons as ends: using a creator’s work purely for one’s own benefit, without consent or compensation, treats the creator as a means rather than as an agent whose rights deserve respect.
  • Utilitarianism is where the debate becomes most contested. The standard justification for copyright is that limited monopoly rights incentivise creation, maximising long-run social welfare (UBC Allard School of Law – Social Media Law 2024). But Waldfogel’s post-Napster analysis directly complicates this: music supply did not decline even as revenue fell sharply after file-sharing became widespread (Waldfogel 2012). If creative output is resilient to piracy, the utilitarian case for strict enforcement weakens – and freer access might produce greater total welfare by reaching more people.
  • These frameworks pull in different directions: Lockean natural rights and Kantian duty point toward strong creator protection, while utilitarian outcomes challenge whether that protection is actually necessary to sustain creative production. The sections below trace how each plays out in practice.

The Case Against Piracy: Harm to Creators

  • Metallica v. Napster (2000): the landmark creator-vs-piracy case. After discovering an unreleased demo circulating on Napster, Metallica identified 335,435 individual users sharing their catalogue and demanded they all be banned. Drummer Lars Ulrich testified before the US Senate, arguing that Napster “never asked us or other artists if we wanted to participate in their business” (Wikipedia contributors 2024a). This is a Kantian argument at its core: Napster used artists’ work as a means without seeking their consent, regardless of whether revenue was actually lost. The case forced Napster into shutdown and shaped digital copyright law for a decade.
  • Lost income: pirated copies represent potential sales that never materialise; most evidence suggests file sharing does displace media sales (Waldfogel 2012) – the Lockean case made concrete: the creator’s labour produces something of value, and piracy takes it without consent or compensation
  • Indie creators are disproportionately hurt: major studios can absorb losses, but small teams rely on every purchase to fund continued development and future projects (GuardingPearSoftware, n.d.)
    • tinyBuild found that their game Punch Club saw a surge in Brazilian piracy only after they launched a Portuguese localisation – users had waited for a translated version rather than paying for the English original (MCV/DEVELOP, n.d.)
  • Reduced incentive to innovate: with limited revenue, studios gravitate toward proven formulas rather than experimental work – empirical research on film confirms that piracy “reduces creative incentives” and negatively impacts the quality of new productions (Initiative for Digital Entertainment Analytics, Carnegie Mellon University, n.d.)

The Case For Piracy: Access and Distribution

  • Censorship and geo-restrictions: when content is banned or refused classification, piracy becomes the only option for consumers
    • Australia’s Classification Board can issue a “Refused Classification” (RC) verdict, banning a game from retail and eliminating all legal distribution channels (Strickland 2019); roughly one in four Australians reported pirating online content in 2022 (SBS News 2023)
    • Hotline Miami 2 (2015) was refused classification in Australia; developer Jonatan Söderström publicly told Australian fans to “just pirate it” – a case where the creator acknowledged the legal system left consumers no legitimate alternative (Engadget 2015)
  • DRM punishes legal buyers more than pirates: CD Projekt Red removed all DRM from their games, arguing copy protection “makes the life of a legal customer more difficult” while pirates bypass it on day one anyway (Lomas 2016) – a utilitarian calculation: the harm imposed on legitimate buyers outweighs any protection gained
    • The Witcher 3 sold nearly 10 million copies across platforms despite being released without copy protection (Purchese 2016)
    • Co-founder Marcin Iwiński: “We believe in the carrot, not the stick. We cannot force people to buy things – we can only convince them to do it.” (Lomas 2016)
  • Piracy as a path to legitimate consumption: in lower-income regions, young people who pirate games often become paying customers once they have disposable income (Purchese 2016)

Ethical Tensions and Nuances

  • Does creator size change the moral calculus? Pirating from a billion-dollar studio is structurally different from pirating from a two-person indie team, even if the act is the same (GuardingPearSoftware, n.d.) – this intuition sits uneasily with Kantian ethics, where the wrongness of an act is independent of the target’s wealth or market position
  • When creators endorse piracy: the Hotline Miami 2 case shows the creator/pirate relationship is not always adversarial – sometimes creators view piracy as preferable to censorship (Strickland 2019)
  • The post-Napster paradox: the recorded music industry saw dramatic revenue decline after Napster, yet the quantity of critically recognised new music did not decline – raising questions about what “harm to creators” actually means in practice (Waldfogel 2012)
  • When enforcement itself causes harm: Nintendo represents the opposite extreme from CD Projekt Red – aggressively removing fan games, issuing around 20,000 GitHub takedown requests per year, and claiming millions of YouTube videos through Content ID (Nintendo Life 2024). The justification is a moral rights argument: creators must control how their work is used to protect integrity and reputation. But critics argue this chills the fan creativity that sustains Nintendo’s cultural reach. Moral rights, taken to their logical conclusion, can become a tool that harms the broader creative ecosystem they were designed to protect.
  • Moral rights and piracy: distributing modified or cracked versions without attribution infringes moral rights even when it causes no direct economic harm (Wikipedia contributors 2024b)

Conclusion

The creator’s perspective on piracy resists a simple verdict. Lockean property rights and Kantian respect for consent both support strong creator protection, and the Metallica case and indie developer stories show that real harm occurs – particularly where economic survival is at stake. Yet the utilitarian evidence complicates the picture: creative supply proved resilient even after Napster, and cases like Hotline Miami 2 and CD Projekt Red show that creators themselves sometimes find piracy preferable to the alternatives on offer. The morally relevant factor may not be piracy per se, but context: who is pirating, from whom, under what conditions, and with what legitimate alternatives available. A framework that treats all piracy as equivalent – whether from a teenager in a low-income country or a bulk commercial operation – misses the ethical distinctions that actually matter.
