The Creator’s Perspective

Author

Andreas Bogossian

Introduction

Piracy is often talked about from the perspective of consumers, but creators sit at the ethical core of the discussion. Creators invest time, money and creative labor into their work and piracy directly affects the return on that investment. The ethical question from the creators’ perspective is: does the public’s interest in access outweigh the creator’s right to control and profit from their work? There are two main rights of the creators that piracy is challenging: economic rights (compensation for use, transferable) and moral rights (authorship and integrity, permanent). Both of these rights are recognized under the Berne Convention (World Intellectual Property Organization, 1886).

Ethical Frameworks

There are three important philosophical frameworks when looking at piracy from the perspective of the creator:

  • Locke’s labour theory: creators who invest labour in their work have a natural property right to it. Piracy takes that product without consent, which is a violation of their property right (Locke, 1689).
  • Kantian deontology: piracy fails the universalisability test (if everyone pirated, creation would collapse) and treats creators as means rather than as agents whose rights deserve respect (UBC Allard School of Law – Social Media Law, 2024).
  • Utilitarianism: copyright gives creators a limited monopoly to recoup their investment, which in theory funds future creation and maximises long-term benefit of both creators and consumers (UBC Allard School of Law – Social Media Law, 2024).

The Case Against Piracy: Harm to Creators

One of the most famous creator-vs-piracy cases was Metallica v. Napster in 2000. Napster was a popular file sharing platform at the time and Metallica identified 335,435 users sharing their catalogue without consent and sued Napster into shutdown. This case is Kantian at its core: Napster used artists’ work as a means for profit without their consent. It is also Lockean in consequence: the product of creators’ labour was taken without compensation, a direct violation of their property right.

“[Napster] never asked us or other artists if we wanted to participate in their business”

— Lars Ulrich, US Senate testimony, 2000

The harm to creators extends beyond landmark legal cases. Waldfogel found that the number of new music releases did not decline even as revenue fell sharply after file-sharing became widespread (Waldfogel, 2012). The reasoning behind this was that creators had already shifted to live performance, merchandise and licensing. These are sources of income that piracy does not directly affect. Spotify pays $0.003–$0.005 per stream ($3,000–$5,000 per million streams (Ditto Music, 2024)), while a single band tee at $35 is worth the equivalent of approximately 9,000 streams (atVenu, 2023). This directly challenges the utilitarian case for strict enforcement. The financial harm to sales is real, but the harm to creation itself is less obvious.

Another interesting question is whether the creator size and popularity affect the ethics of pirating. Pirating from a billion-dollar studio is structurally different from pirating from a two-person indie team. This viewpoint goes against Kantian ethics, where the wrongness of an act is independent of the target’s wealth.

The Case For Piracy: Access and Distribution

Even though piracy can cause monetary harm to creators, not all creators oppose it either. When content is banned or refused classification, piracy becomes the only option. For example, the game Hotline Miami 2 (2015) was refused classification in Australia. After the news broke, the developer Jonatan Söderström publicly told Australian fans to “just pirate it” (Engadget, 2015).

There are also many cases where digital rights management (DRM) punishes legal buyers more than pirates. CD Projekt Red removed all DRM from their games, arguing copy protection “makes the life of a legal consumer more difficult” while pirates bypass it on day one anyway (Lomas, 2016). This is a utilitarian calculation: rather than restricting access, they chose to offer a better experience to paying customers and win over pirates through quality. Their flagship game, The Witcher 3, sold nearly 10 million copies without copy protection (Purchese, 2016).

“We believe in the carrot, not the stick. We cannot force people to buy things – we can only convince them to do it.”

— Marcin Iwiński, co-founder of CD Projekt Red (Lomas, 2016)

In both cases, the creators who embraced piracy – or at least refused to fight it – argued that a better product is a more effective response than restriction.
