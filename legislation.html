The Government’s Perspective

Author

Son Tong

Introduction: The Legislative History of Digital Piracy

To understand how governments came to adopt their current positions on digital piracy, we first trace the history of intellectual property law and its adaptation to the digital age. The key milestones in the modern era, particularly relevant to piracy, are as follows:
- 1886: Berne Convention The Berne Convention, widely recognized as the first major multilateral international copyright treaty, introduced the principles of national treatment and minimum protection terms (Copyright Clearance Center, n.d.).
- 1994: WTO TRIPS Agreement The Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS) Agreement established guidelines for the protection of digital works and enforcement in the context of global trade.
- 1996: WIPO Copyright Treaty As works increasingly existed in digital formats, traditional copyright frameworks struggled to address these new issues. The World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) responded with the WIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT), explicitly extending copyright into the digital environment.
- 1998: DMCA The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) made it illegal to circumvent technological measures used to prevent unauthorized access to or copying of copyrighted works, particularly books, movies, videos, video games, and computer programs.
- 2001: EU Copyright Directive The EU’s Information Society Directive (2001/29/EC) harmonized copyright law across member states for the digital age, implementing the WIPO Internet Treaties into European law. It granted rights holders exclusive control over reproduction and online communication, while allowing member states to adopt limited exceptions for education and research.
- 2011-2012: SOPA/PIPA Debates The Stop Online Piracy Act (SOPA) and its Senate counterpart PROTECT IP Act (PIPA) were defeated by an enormous online campaign, culminating in an Internet Blackout on January 18, 2012. This significantly reshaped the government policy debate on enforcement overreach.
- 2013/2016: Marrakesh Treaty Adopted in 2013 and entering into force in 2016, the Marrakesh Treaty requires signatory governments to create copyright exceptions permitting the production and exchange of accessible-format works such as Braille or audio books for visually impaired persons.
- 2019-2021: Copyright Modernization Laws Around this period, many countries updated their frameworks for the new era. The EU’s Digital Single Market Directive (2019/790) was the most consequential, introducing platform upload filters and new press publishers’ rights against tech aggregators. The U.S. passed the Copyright Alternative in Small-Claims Enforcement (CASE) Act in 2020.

This legislative history shows that copyright law did not spontaneously emerge. Each milestone was a direct response to new technologies and shifting power dynamics. Therefore, the laws governments enforce today are the cumulative product of decades of negotiation between creators, industries, and the public.

Ethical Frameworks

  • Social Contract Theory: Moral obligations arise from agreements, explicit or implicit, between citizens and their institutions (Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, n.d.). The key question is what rules people would freely and fairly agree to.
  • Deontological Ethics: Actions are judged by whether they conform to universal duties, regardless of consequences (Khasanova, 2024). Kant’s Categorical Imperative instructs agents to act only on maxims that could be universalized without contradiction (Korsgaard, 2012).
  • Consequentialism: The view that the moral rightness of an action depends entirely on its consequences. Thus, outcomes (economic, social, and security-related) are what matter morally (Card & Smith, 2020).

Copyright as a Social Contract

Copyright is not an absolute property right but a social contract granted to creators in exchange for eventual public access through the public domain. This reflects a Lockean intuition that creators naturally deserve the fruits of their intellectual labour, balanced against a concern for the collective good and public access (Fisher, 2001). Piracy breaks this bargain prematurely because it deprives creators of their entitlement before the public benefit phase arrives.

However, the contract binds both sides. A government that enforces creator rights while failing to ensure affordable public access has also broken its half of the deal. The social contract framework therefore supports enforcement and demands equity.

Deontological Analysis: Duties and Rights

Kant’s Categorical Imperative provides a powerful argument against piracy. If one adopts the maxim “I will use copyrighted content without paying whenever I prefer not to,” the practice is impossible to universalize consistently, as it depends on a system of rights and obligations from which the agent simultaneously seeks exemption. On this view, piracy is wrong as a matter of principle rather than merely because of its economic consequences.

Yet Kant also constrains how governments may enforce. The Formula of Humanity states that you should treat humanity always as an end, and never merely as a means to an end (Korsgaard, 2012). Hence, criminally prosecuting economically vulnerable end-users risks treating them as deterrence instruments rather than persons of dignity. Deontological ethics thus supports targeting commercial-scale piracy while advocating for restraint in actions against individual end-users who are genuinely in need.

Consequentialist Analysis: Weighing Welfare

A utilitarian analysis of piracy must consider both the harms and benefits it generates. On the harm side:
- Economic losses: Global online piracy costs the U.S. economy at least $29.2 billion in lost revenue annually, with 200,000-500,000 jobs lost and $47.5-115.3 billion in GDP impact (U.S. Chamber of Commerce, 2019).
- Tax shortfalls: Pirated goods bypass income, sales, and VAT contributions, directly eroding public budgets.
- Cybersecurity harm: Piracy sites are primary sources of malware, ransomware, and spyware. Users with piracy apps are three times more likely to suffer infections, with a significant impact in sensitive sectors such as healthcare and finance (Digital Citizens Alliance, 2021). The section The Consumer’s Perspective provides a detailed account of the security risks involved.
- Innovation disincentive: Since piracy reduces creator earnings, it could discourage future production and innovation, which ultimately harms the public (Danaher et al., 2013).

However, consequentialism does not yield a straightforward verdict. Piracy also generates welfare: students in low-income countries access textbooks they could never afford; individuals reach cultural works priced beyond their means. Pistorius and Mwim (Pistorius & Mwim, 2019) warn that aggressive technological protections “may hinder data literacy, access to works, teaching and learning, particularly in developing communities.” The economic drivers behind this such as subscription fragmentation, purchasing power gaps, and regional pricing failures, are examined in detail in The Consumer’s Perspective. Therefore, based on the utilitarian framework, harms exist on both sides, and the optimal policy must weigh them carefully.

Policy Implications

Across all four frameworks, the analysis converges on a balanced framework rather than full prohibition:
- Proportionate enforcement: Focus criminal penalties on commercial-scale piracy and avoid prosecuting individuals driven by inaccessibility.
- Demand-side reform: Expand affordable legal alternatives, as evidence suggests access reduces piracy more effectively than enforcement alone (Danaher et al., 2013). The commercial case for this, illustrated by Steam’s success in Russia, is explored in The Consumer’s Perspective.
- International equity reform: Push for stronger educational fair-use exceptions and development-friendly treaty terms within WIPO and WTO.
- Copyright reform: Ensure copyright terms and exceptions genuinely serve the public interest, not just incumbent industries.

Conclusion

From a government’s perspective, digital piracy is ethically problematic, yet no framework grants governments unconditional moral authority to enforce without reflection. Consequentialism demands that access gains be weighed alongside enforcement benefits; social contract theory demands that both sides of the copyright bargain be honored. Taken together, the frameworks demand not just the prohibition of piracy, but the construction of a system in which legal access to digital products is fairly available to everyone. Until that system exists, the government’s moral standing to condemn piracy unconditionally remains incomplete.
